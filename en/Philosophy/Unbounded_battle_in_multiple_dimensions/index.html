Unbounded battle in multiple dimensions

@2026/04/24

Creativity — the kind this essay is about — doesn't live inside one head. It emerges between minds as a response to a provocation: a move in a game no single player controls.

Gosh, I love this scene from the Legion:

The main protagonist with psychic superpowers is in a floating tank, connected to some sort of amplifier, which should help him find the Monk. And as he tracks him through the world, two other psychics stop him. And so, they battle. With dance.

I can hardly imagine a better visual metaphor. All of them are strong psychics, and the director decided that it would be better to show us the battle of creativity instead of some "action" scene where they battle with invisible forces. If you know what this is about, you can see how they all telepathically allocate people from the pool of dancers near them and fight with dance creativity.

Can you imagine several god-like people fighting together? When they can do whatever they want, the only thing that really matters is the creativity, and ability to properly identify the game and choose the right strategy. To adapt. To invent your own game.

Chess metaphore 🔗

The second Sherlock Holmes movie contains a famous chess scene, where two super geniuses battle with each other while playing chess, and their friends and allies meanwhile do stuff in the background.

But that, although multidimensional, is still a bounded game of chess. This isn't:

Two people playing chess against each other, making rules on the go, as they please. Quite fascinating, really.

The Human (book) 🔗

Neal Asher described in his book The Human battle of several fractions;

There is the human (Trike), as the book title says. There is Orlandine, who is an amalgam of AI and human (so-called haiman). There are AIs. And then there is the Jain, an ancient, five milion years old alien who is freed from their prison in space-time. When he comes, he screams his challenge.

Within the weapons platform, the Client saw something like the Jain shriek emerging. It consisted of open-ended formulae, incomplete molecular models, but also the elements of a language, familiar because it resembled that of the Species. She began to design responses—to solve the formulae and complete the molecules, and to answer the questions in that ancient tongue. These she transmitted to her remote. With each response the particular "question" folded away to leave a hole in the growing model.
  • ASHER, Neal. The Human. New York: Night Shade Books, 2020. ISBN 978-1-949102-24-6. Chapter 2.
Diana was about to reply when the mother ship screamed again. It went right through her, through her mind and through the mind of her AI. It filled up every com channel and made a nonsense of all the coding changes and other attempts at shielding. As before, she felt it in her bones, and this time by some form of induction it even became audible. But then another shriek answered and countered it—almost like antisound or white noise across the informational spectrum. It ate up and converted the first scream to something ineffectual, collapsed its structure, solved its formulae and replied in kind.
  • ASHER, Neal. The Human. New York: Night Shade Books, 2020. ISBN 978-1-949102-24-6. Chapter 11.

Picture it like this: it's not sound. It's an information tsunami that exists simultaneously across every communication layer at once — radio, laser, induction, virtual warfare channels, even ones you didn't know existed. Every medium you can transmit data through is suddenly flooded.

And the key part — it's not just noise or a jammer. The scream has structure. It contains:

  • incomplete mathematical formulae that demand to be solved
  • unfinished molecular models
  • fragments of a language

It's actually an enormous invitation to dialogue disguised as an attack. Like someone shrieking in your face, but the shrieking is actually a cipher, a riddle, and an invitation to a death dance all at once.

And the response? You have to understand the scream, solve its formulae, complete the molecules, answer in the correct language — and then send the whole thing back as an anti-signal that collapses the original structure. Like someone handing you an equation and you have to find the solution in real time and throw it back, or it eats you alive.

But notice what the scream actually contains — incomplete formulae, fragments of language, an invitation to reply. It looks like an attack, but the structure is a something else. Asher dressed it in cosmic weaponry because otherwise the novel wouldn't work. But the structure is older than weapons.

The other interesting thing about this battle is that it is led on all the available levels.

The first level is the combat between the spaceships, which, although described in detail, is not the main part of the battle.

The second level is the physical fight between two main protagonists of the battle.

The third level is the electronic warfare they are leading against each other while they fight. And there is nano-level, steal-your-opponent’s-robots-level, chemical-level, and psychological-level.

We have several intelligent entities battling each other using knowledge and information. And not only information, but also the ability to process that information, to understand it, to find some sort of creative solution for their problem.

Creativity and information processing are more essential than raw power.

Tag war 🔗

Ages ago I wrote a blog post on abclinuxu and tagged it. Within a short while someone came along, deleted my tags, and started adding all sorts of childish nonsense, like:

  • BystroNoFuxGotBurned,
  • bystronofux
  • VirginAlert
  • BystroFuxIsBrokeLoser
  • bystronof
  • BystroNoFuxIsIdiot
  • and so on

(My handle there was Bystroushaak; the guy riffed it into BystroFux — fox + fuck — and the virgin-jab variant BystroNoFux, i.e. "doesn't fux". All of this is translated from Czech to English, so take it with a grain of salt.)

My first reaction was of course to go delete it (manually), which worked about the way you'd expect; not at all. The guy, on the other hand, started adding things like BystroNoFuxDeletesTilDeath, BystroNoFuxTakesSelfTooSeriously, and my all-time favorite WhereAreYourTagsTemplar (wut :D) and so on.

Within a short while I realized this was pointless, so I went and wrote a script that deleted his and replied (also somewhat childishly) with my tag IveGotAScriptForThatYouScab along with my original (meaningful) tags.

As you'd expect, this only escalated things. For a while we exchanged insults and messages via tags like this, until the guy also wrote a script (and added ThanksForTheIdea). It also had the downside that if someone came along and added a tag in good faith, my script would delete it anyway.

So there was nothing else left to do except to make two scripts: one UI for management, the other for super-fast re-tagging every few seconds whenever it detected a change. And of course to add the tag WorthIt. Every so often I'd check what the script had added and removed, I would allow the normal tags, and throw the deranged ones onto the banlist.

In the end my script ran a bit faster than his, but even so we kept dueling like this for another half year (the guy adding BystroNoFuxxxIsNoLifer, and deleting my own), and I kept running my own script for years. That's why most of my blogs had the tag ProtectedByTagManager, which I only gave up on when I left abclinuxu.

Of course I could have just let it go, but .. I enjoyed it. Moronic tags turned into a squabble and a script-writing duel. The one thing that bothered me about it was that this was a communication protocol over which we had both created and implemented a chat. And my attempt to say anything didn't work, because from the other side this wasn't a chat. It was an attack.

Pingback 🔗

Yeah you ping my direct reports! You see what happens! .. They’ll ping you right back!

One of the internal motivations behind everything I've ever done in the field of programming languages and programming in general can be traced back to The Great and Secret Show by Clive Barker.

There's something about the backstory of the book's main protagonist that stuck with me; he works at a post office, where his job is to open letters and look for valuables inside. He can't help himself and starts reading the letters, gradually figuring out how the world really works, and gains access to the Art; basically magic for controlling the world.

This idea — that there are hidden communication channels in the world — has been coming back to me forever (see Paranoid theory - The Society of NemosBystroushaakBystroushaak). Yeah, sure, it's a bit paranoid, but are you even looking?

For example, once I noticed that someone was periodically scraping a file with the list of books I've finished reading, which I keep posted on my website. It didn't surprise me; that's the whole point of why I save it in DSV format. So it can be machine-read and processed.

I looked into the logs and saw that someone had actually understood, and from the referrer it was sometimes obvious that these were people trying to communicate .. something. Among others links to their socials:

  • https://kind.social/@viralstitch
  • https://git.eeqj.de/sneak

And so on. The obvious question is whether it's worth communicating with such a person. A scrape = a silent challenge; a reply = turning a log entry into a conversation. Next time, throw the invitation to dialogue back at them instead of content of the file, establish a connection. Or go back, look at what's on the IP address they're coming from; maybe there's a challenge waiting there too.

To this day I don't know why I never did it.

The world of Software 🔗

All of this reminds me again of something I once wrote down in Jak a proč se stát programátorem;

You can't grab a program, shout it down, or overpower it by force. You interact with it through dimensions the human body doesn't give you access to; no touch, no hearing, no voice. The only way in is to grow into them: you learn to use and build tools that feel and function inside those dimensions, and then you wire those tools into your brain — whether via alerts, visualizations, or simply by manually running something from the console and checking the result.

Every tool shapes you just as much as you shape it; another nerve ending stretched out into the info-sphere. Programming for me was never about writing code — it was the effort to become something more, so that there was even someone and something to meet in that dimension at all. An ape learns to read and write; a programmer learns to shape thoughts into a form a machine can execute, and then through them stretches the tentacles (the Software) to places the body can't reach.

And this is exactly where I circle back to the whole essay: if creativity arises between minds across dimensions, then software is the medium where you choose how many of them you can feel and respond in. Every new sense = another room where a challenge can be met. Do you realize that when the game stretches across enough dimensions, it stops being a duel and becomes a dance? And the best dancers today have no body at all.

Creativity 🔗

Overall, this makes me reflect on creativity.

When I was in elementary school, I experienced something that I still don't fully understand to this day;

The music teacher brought in many different Indian musical instruments to the classroom. Most of them I wouldn't be able to name. It was some kind of "drum," "flute," "weird harmonica," and so on. Then she said, "Take an instrument and start playing." Most of the kids grabbed an instrument. I didn't.

Then she took a drum and started playing it. "Whoever wants can join in, play whatever you want." And people started joining in and playing whatever they wanted.

At first, it sounded weird, but within a very short amount of time, it started sounding good. Someone who grabbed the unusual flute threw in some melodies, and people started improvising. I don't remember the music very well, but I remember the feeling. It was the most powerful thing I’ve heard up to this time. It still resonates, more than 20 years later.

When it ended, we all looked at each other in disbelief - how did we just do that? How did that even happen?

Emergent music, created from nothing.

My Jain broadcast 🔗

In Neal Asher's universe, Jain entities don't attack on arrival — they broadcast a challenge across every available dimension at once: chemically, via programs, via radio, via nanotech. It's an invitation, not a strike: show me you're here in full bandwidth.

Everything above is one of these broadcasts at a smaller scale. Tag wars, fecal poetry, dance battles, emergent music from a roomful of kids with borrowed Indian instruments, a scrape-and-reply over a list of finished books. Someone throws a challenge across dimensions; someone answers in an unexpected one; the loop begins. Unbounded because the medium is free — the creative move is to switch layers, not to play better within the same one.

But all of this happened in the past. This blog is my broadcast, where I am broadcasting the evidence of the game as the game itself. And I never understood why. It made no sense. I had this blog in my wiki since 2020, and I had no idea what to do with it. I’ve tried to finish it probably fifty different times, and I just couldn’t.

Then I realized; there was never anything wrong with the blog itself; the wrong part was the intended audience. There was None. This blog was never meant for you, it was always, from the beginning, for them. And they are so fucking smart, it almost scares me when I am not laughing in pure pleasure. This essay is my Jain broadcast, except it is not a challenge for combat; but if you are the target audience, you already know exactly what it is, don’t you?
