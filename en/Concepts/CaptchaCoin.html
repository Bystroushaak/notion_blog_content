CaptchaCoin

@2020/06/09

The concept of something I called "CaptchaCoin" around 2012:

The original idea was relatively simpleâ€”I needed to automate solving captchas on websites from which I was downloading content. At the time, there were services offering this, but they were all quite expensive for my budget as a poor student.

I thought it would be cool if there were an automated system, similar to cryptocurrency, where you could manually solve captchas today and earn some credit. This credit could then be used in the future to have the system automatically solve captchas for you.

This would be a kind of time multiplexing. For example, if I solve 10 captchas today, I would earn 5 captcha coins, which means I could redeem 5 captchas at some future point.

I intentionally wanted the system to give less credit for more work to avoid issues with network size and to ensure that as the number of users grows, it would remain realistic to use the earned credits. This would work like the "difficulty" in Bitcoin but in a static and simpler manner.

The whole idea was motivated by the fact that I had no problem sitting down and solving 100 captchasâ€”I had plenty of time. However, I didn't want to solve a captcha every time my script needed to download something.

CacheCoin

Over time, I realized that this concept could be generalized, not just for captchas but for any kind of resource I have. An obvious example would be computing power (CPU), memory (RAM), and disk space.

Let's say I'm not using my computer; I could install CacheCoin and offer its resources to the network. Later, when I need computing power, I could use the CacheCoins I earned from renting out my resources to get it back.

Eventually, I thought this could also work with lending books. I have a book, I list it on the network as available, and if someone wants it, I give it to them. I earn 1 book CacheCoin, and the person who takes the book pays with CacheCoins. When I want a book, I can pay for it in the same way.

Of course, the whole idea is a bit more complicated, and obviously, I never implemented it. Specifically, for CPU sharing, it would ideally require something like homomorphic encryption, which is a cutting-edge technology and I didn't want to spend another 10 years experimenting with it.

For other resources, it would be hard to make it work without some fine-tuning, and setting the difficulty wouldn't be easy. For example, what does earning 1 book CacheCoin actually mean? Are all books worth the same? That doesn't make much sense. Moreover, there's nothing to prevent someone from emptying a community library, filling the network with old, uninteresting books, and taking all the new, interesting ones. Although market mechanisms might prevent this, I think you get the idea.

However, I still believe that if there were a system where I could reliably time-multiplex a resource without having to interact with anyone and without it costing me any energy, it would have its value.
